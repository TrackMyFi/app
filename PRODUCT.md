# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Primarily the developer (Tom) and a small audience of FIRE-minded people. Users understand financial independence deeply — they know what a savings rate means, why investable net worth differs from total net worth, and what a 4% withdrawal rate implies. They're opening this app deliberately, often daily or weekly, to check their progress toward a specific life goal.

Context: solo, desktop, focused. Not a casual glance — a deliberate check-in.

## Product Purpose

TrackMyFI is a local-first desktop tracker for the FIRE journey. It holds account balances, transactions, paychecks, contributions, budget, and forecast in one place — entirely on your machine, no vendor lock-in.

Success looks like: opening the app, seeing your numbers clearly, understanding where you stand relative to FI, and closing it feeling informed and grounded. The app doesn't need to sell itself; it needs to be trustworthy and satisfying to use every day.

## Brand Personality

Intimate, precise, motivating.

The app knows your real numbers — savings rate, projected FI date, contributions. That's personal. The design should feel like a well-crafted tool you built for yourself: not sterile, not flashy, just honest and considered. Financial independence is a long game; the UI should make the journey feel meaningful, not clinical.

## Anti-references

- **Spreadsheet UI**: dense tabular interfaces that look like Excel reskinned. Every element on screen should feel intentional, not just "the row that was next in the table."
- Gamified personal finance apps (Mint, Personal Capital energy): colorful dashboards built to impress rather than inform.
- Corporate financial institution UI: stiff, conservative, zero personality.

## Design Principles

1. **Your numbers, your story.** Data here is personal — a projected FI date isn't just a number, it's a life target. Design that treats it as such: give meaningful numbers visual weight, let context show.

2. **Clarity without coldness.** Information should be instantly readable but never feel like a report. Warmth comes from spacing, typography weight, and considered color — not gradients or decoration.

3. **Earned density.** Surface what matters at a glance; don't hide depth behind extra clicks. But don't default to tabular layouts just because the data is structured. Tables earn their place; so does every column.

4. **Progress feels real.** Showing someone they're 34% of the way to FI should feel different from showing a generic progress bar. Design interactions that make the journey feel substantial and worth tracking.

5. **Respect the desktop context.** This is a native desktop app opened in a focused session, not a web dashboard skimmed on mobile. Density and precision are appropriate; so are subtle but deliberate interactions.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Good defaults: readable contrast ratios, no color-only information encoding, keyboard-navigable interactions. No hard WCAG level — just choices a thoughtful designer would make.
